## What Is Contractor Payment Collection Software?

Contractor payment collection software lets you send payment links by text or email, accept credit cards and ACH bank transfers in the field or online, and automatically match payments to invoices — without chasing checks or waiting for wires.

The difference from a personal Venmo or Square account: it integrates with your invoicing and accounting, tracks which jobs are paid and which aren't, handles progress payments and retainage, and gives you reporting your accountant can actually use.

According to the 2024 Levelset Construction Payment Report, 84% of contractors have experienced slow payment from clients, and the average contractor waits 54 days to collect on completed work. That gap between when you finish and when you get paid is financed by your working capital — or your credit line.

The core benefits:

- **Faster collection** — clients pay by clicking a link, not hunting for their checkbook
- **Field collection** — accept a card on-site at completion, not 6 weeks later
- **Automatic reconciliation** — payments match to invoices without manual entry
- **Cash flow visibility** — see what's been paid, what's pending, and what's overdue in one dashboard

Contractors who add digital payment options report reducing average collection time from 45–60 days to 7–14 days, and cutting time spent on payment follow-up by 3–5 hours per week.

## The Real Cost of Checks and Cash

Paper payments feel free. Here's what they actually cost:

**Float and opportunity cost.** A check mailed on Friday arrives Tuesday, clears Thursday. That's 6 days of float on every payment. For a contractor doing $1.5M annually with 45-day average collection, $185,000 is tied up in receivables at any given time. If you're borrowing on a line of credit at 8% to cover that gap, the interest cost alone is $14,800 per year.

**Admin time.** Your office manager spends 4–6 hours per week on payment-related tasks: generating invoices, following up on unpaid ones, depositing checks, reconciling the bank, calling clients who haven't responded. At $22/hour, that's $5,000–$6,800 per year just in labor — before the cost of missed payments.

**Bad debt.** A 2023 Levelset survey found that 28% of contractors wrote off at least one bad debt in the past year. The average write-off was $6,200. Contractors who collect payment by credit card have a 60–70% lower bad debt rate — chargebacks are disputable, but they at least create a paper trail and an enforcement mechanism.

**The check that never comes.** You finished the job 8 weeks ago. The client says the check is in the mail. It's not. You follow up 3 more times. Eventually you either write it off or start thinking about small claims court. A payment link sent at job completion, with a "pay now" button, gets paid at a dramatically higher rate than an invoice mailed after the fact.

## What Contractors Actually Need From Payment Software

Not every payment feature matters equally. Here's what moves the needle for contractors:

**1. Text and email payment links.** Send a link when you complete the job. The client clicks it, enters their card or bank info, and you see the payment within minutes. No waiting for a check. No "I'll drop it off." This single feature reduces collection time more than any other.

**2. Mobile card readers.** On-site payment at job completion. For service contractors (HVAC, plumbing, electrical), collecting at the door is standard. For remodelers and GCs, collecting a completion payment on-site instead of waiting for a mailed check eliminates weeks of float.

**3. ACH bank transfer (low or no fee).** Credit cards cost 2.6–2.9% per transaction. On a $25,000 job, that's $650–$725 out of your pocket. ACH transfers cost $0.25–$1.50 flat, regardless of amount. For large invoices, ACH is the right option — and your software should make it easy for clients to pay by bank transfer.

**4. Progress payment and retainage tracking.** You're billing $40,000 over 4 phases with 10% retainage held until completion. Your payment software needs to handle partial payments, track what's been released, and clearly show your client what they owe at each milestone.

**5. QuickBooks or accounting sync.** Payments collected in your payment tool should post to your accounting system automatically. If someone's re-entering payments manually, you're paying twice for the same transaction.

## How to Choose Contractor Payment Collection Software

**1. Fee structure.** Every tool charges 2.6–2.9% for credit cards. The real differentiator is ACH — some tools charge nothing, some charge $1–1.50 per transfer, some charge 0.8%. For large invoices, ACH cost is the number that matters.

**2. Client experience.** Can your client pay by clicking a link in a text message? Or do they need to create an account? Friction kills payment speed. The simpler it is for the client, the faster you collect.

**3. Integration with your invoicing tool.** If you're already invoicing in QuickBooks, FreshBooks, or Jobber, pick a payment tool that connects directly. Manually reconciling payments against invoices is exactly what you're trying to eliminate.

**4. Mobile card reader.** Do you need to accept cards in the field? If yes, make sure the app supports it and the reader is included or cheap. Square's reader is free; others charge $49–99.

**5. Progress and retainage support.** If you do multi-phase billing, confirm the tool handles partial payments and retainage before committing. Most generic payment processors don't.

**You DON'T need dedicated payment software if:**

- You collect full payment upfront via cash or check and have no collection problems
- All your clients are commercial with net-30 terms and pay reliably on that schedule
- You do fewer than 10 jobs per year and your admin handles it in under 2 hours per week

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the best way for contractors to collect payment?

The fastest way is a payment link sent by text or email at job completion — clients click it, pay by card or ACH, and you see the money within 1–2 business days. For large invoices over $10,000, push clients toward ACH to keep fees under $10. For on-site service work, a mobile card reader at job completion beats chasing a check by weeks.

### How much does it cost contractors to accept credit cards?

Standard processing fees are 2.6–2.9% + $0.10–$0.30 per transaction for all major platforms (Square, Stripe, QuickBooks Payments). On a $5,000 invoice, that's $130–$145. On a $25,000 invoice, it's $650–$725. ACH bank transfer is far cheaper — Stripe charges 0.8% capped at $5, QuickBooks charges 1% capped at $10.

### Should I pass credit card fees on to clients?

You can — it's legal in most states with proper disclosure. Most contractors who add a 3% surcharge for card payments find that clients choose ACH instead, which costs you almost nothing. If you add a surcharge, disclose it on your contract and invoice before the client pays.

### What's the difference between Square and Stripe for contractors?

Square is simpler to set up and better for in-person and small-invoice collection — free monthly cost, free card reader, clean client experience. Stripe has better ACH pricing and more flexibility for custom workflows, but requires more technical setup and isn't built for construction billing out of the box. Most small contractors do better with Square; larger or tech-forward shops may prefer Stripe's economics.

### How do I get clients to pay faster?

Send the invoice the day you finish the job, not a week later. Include a payment link in the invoice email and text. Offer ACH as a no-surcharge option. Set clear payment terms in your contract (due on receipt or net-7, not net-30). For large projects, use progress billing so you're never chasing one large final payment — you've already collected most of the money before the punch list.

### Can I accept partial payments and retainage with payment apps?

Square and Stripe handle partial payments by letting clients pay any amount. HouseCall Pro and QuickBooks Payments support partial invoice payments natively with milestone tracking. For formal retainage management — holding 10% until completion and releasing it against a final invoice — QuickBooks Online Payments or a construction-specific platform like Procore handles this most cleanly.

### What happens if a client disputes a credit card payment?

You'll receive a chargeback notice and have the opportunity to respond with documentation. Keep signed contracts, completion photos, client-signed change orders, and written communication. Contractors with strong documentation win the majority of disputes. This is another reason GPS-verified job photos and digital signatures matter — they're your evidence in a chargeback.

## Preguntas Frecuentes — Software de Cobro de Pagos para Contratistas

### ¿Cuál es la mejor forma de cobrar pagos como contratista?

La forma más rápida es un enlace de pago enviado por texto o correo electrónico al terminar el trabajo. Para facturas grandes (más de $10,000), orienta al cliente hacia transferencia ACH — el cargo máximo es $5–10 en lugar del 2.9% por tarjeta de crédito. Para trabajos de servicio en campo, un lector de tarjetas móvil al terminar el trabajo elimina semanas de espera.

### ¿Cuánto cuesta aceptar tarjetas de crédito como contratista?

Las tarifas estándar son 2.6–2.9% más $0.10–$0.30 por transacción. En una factura de $25,000, eso es $650–$725. La transferencia bancaria ACH es mucho más barata: Stripe cobra 0.8% con un máximo de $5, QuickBooks cobra 1% con un máximo de $10.

### ¿Puedo trasladar los cargos de tarjeta de crédito al cliente?

Sí, es legal en la mayoría de los estados con la divulgación adecuada. La mayoría de los contratistas que agregan un recargo del 3% encuentran que los clientes eligen ACH en su lugar, lo que casi no tiene costo.
