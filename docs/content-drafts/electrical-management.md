# Best Electrical Business Management Software for 2025

Electrical contractors have specific software needs that generic field service platforms often miss: permit tracking across multiple jurisdictions, panel inspection documentation, code compliance records, and the fact that your diagnostic calls take longer and are harder to price than most other trades.

The right software for an electrical company isn't just job scheduling and invoicing — it's a platform that handles the full workflow from dispatch to permit close-out, with pricing tools that help your techs sell service upgrades without underselling complex work.

This guide compares the best electrical business management tools, with honest notes on pricing, who each one fits, and what it actually takes to get your team using it.

## What Electrical Contractors Need That Generic Software Misses

### Trade-Specific Workflow Requirements

Electrical work has workflow requirements that general service software wasn't built for:

**Permit management:** Most electrical work requires permits, and permit requirements vary by city, county, and project type. Your software needs to track permit status — applied, approved, inspected, closed — across every active job. Missing a permit close-out creates liability.

**Inspection scheduling:** Rough-in inspections, final inspections, and special inspections all need to be scheduled around your crew schedule and the inspector's availability. Tracking these manually creates delays and callbacks.

**Code compliance documentation:** When a customer or owner asks for documentation of code compliance on a panel upgrade or service entrance work, you need job records that show what was done, by whom, and when. This is also what protects you when work is questioned years later.

**Panel and equipment records:** Knowing the history of every panel you've touched — what was upgraded, what was found, what was quoted and declined — is both a customer service advantage and a liability protection measure.

### The Three Roles Electrical Software Plays

1. **Dispatch and scheduling** — getting the right tech to the right job with the right parts
2. **Customer and job records** — maintaining complete histories of every customer, location, and service call
3. **Financial management** — quoting, invoicing, and collecting payment efficiently

The tools below differ significantly in how well they handle each of these roles.

## Choosing the Right Platform for Your Shop Size

**Solo operator (1 tech):** Jobber at $29/month. You need scheduling, invoicing, and payment — nothing more complex. Spend the savings on marketing.

**2–4 techs ($300K–$1M):** HouseCall Pro at the entry tier or Jobber's mid-tier. HouseCall Pro's flat pricing is better as you add techs; Jobber is simpler if you want minimal setup.

## Making the Transition: What to Expect

**The customer database migration** is the most time-intensive part of any platform switch. If you have 1,000+ past customers in your old system or spreadsheets, plan 8–16 hours of data cleanup before import. Don't migrate dirty data — fix addresses, remove duplicates, and standardize naming before the import.

**Price book setup** (ServiceTitan and HouseCall Pro): building your flat-rate pricing before go-live is essential. If your techs go live without a complete price book, they'll revert to manual pricing — and you've paid for a platform you're not using. Commit to building out your 50 most common services before your launch date.

**Common transition mistakes for electrical contractors:**
- Going live during busy season — transition during January or August when call volume is lower
- Not training techs on the mobile app before their first live job
- Migrating without a complete customer history — techs need to see past job records from day one
- Underestimating permit tracking setup — configure permit types and required fields before go-live
