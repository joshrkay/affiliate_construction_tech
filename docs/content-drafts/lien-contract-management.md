# Best Lien and Contract Management Software for Contractors (2025)

Miss a preliminary notice deadline by one day and you've waived your lien rights in most states. Miss the lien filing window — typically 60–90 days from your last day on the job — and you become an unsecured creditor. That means you're last in line if the project owner goes bankrupt, gets foreclosed on, or just decides not to pay.

Lien rights are the most powerful collection tool a contractor has. They're also the most time-sensitive and the most state-specific. The software that tracks these deadlines automatically is how companies stop losing six-figure receivables to paperwork errors.

This guide compares the top lien and contract management tools, with honest notes on who each one fits and what it actually takes to protect your receivables.

## Why Lien Management Requires Software

### The Deadline Problem Is Worse Than You Think

Construction lien law varies by state in ways that catch contractors off guard:

**Preliminary notices:** Required before you can file a lien in most states. In California, Texas, Florida, and Colorado, a preliminary notice (also called a "Notice to Owner" or "Notice to Contractor" depending on the state) must be sent within a specific window after first furnishing labor or materials — often 20 days. Miss it, and you've forfeited lien rights for the work delivered before the notice.

**Sub-tier requirements:** First-tier subs have different notice requirements than material suppliers and sub-subs. A sub-sub who doesn't send a preliminary notice in a mandatory-notice state loses lien rights entirely, regardless of whether the GC knows they're on the job.

**Lien filing deadlines:** Typically 60–90 days from last furnishing date, but the window varies by state, project type (private vs. public), and claimant tier. Public projects have bond claim deadlines instead of lien deadlines — often shorter.

**Lien waiver traps:** A broad unconditional lien waiver signed before payment clears is irrevocable in most states, even if the check bounces. The language in a waiver matters more than most contractors realize.

Manual tracking of these deadlines across multiple active projects and multiple states is where money gets lost.

### The Two Problems: Lien Management vs. Contract Management

These are often bundled but are genuinely different:

**Lien management** covers preliminary notices, lien filings, lien waivers, and bond claims. The goal is protecting your right to get paid.

**Contract management** covers contract creation, review, execution, change orders, and compliance tracking. The goal is clear scope, clear terms, and a documented record when disputes arise.

Some platforms (Levelset) focus on lien management. Others (DocuSign CLM) focus on contract management. A few attempt both. Knowing which problem is costing you more money determines where to start.

## The Lien Waiver Trap Every GC Should Know

Lien waivers are the most misunderstood document in construction. Two things that get contractors in trouble:

**Broad unconditional waivers:** An unconditional lien waiver waives your right to lien permanently, even if payment doesn't clear. Some owners and GCs include unconditional waiver language in "conditional" waiver forms — read the language, not just the title.

**Joint check trap:** If you receive a joint check from a GC (payable to you and your supplier), you may need to notify your supplier and endorse the check within a specific window or face lien waiver issues. The rules vary by state.

Software like Levelset includes state-compliant waiver templates that use the correct conditional vs. unconditional language. Using a template from Google doesn't guarantee compliance.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is a preliminary notice and do I need to send one?**
A preliminary notice (also called Notice to Owner, Notice to Contractor, or Pre-Lien Notice depending on the state) is a document sent to the property owner and/or GC early in the project that preserves your right to file a mechanic's lien if unpaid. It's mandatory in California, Texas, Florida, Colorado, Arizona, and most other states. In some states (New York, Pennsylvania), it's optional but advisable. Missing a mandatory preliminary notice deadline waives your lien rights for work done before the notice.

**How long do I have to file a mechanic's lien?**
The window varies by state, claimant tier, and project type. Common windows: 60 days (California for subs and suppliers on private projects), 90 days (Texas for most claimants), 120 days (Florida). The clock typically starts from your last day furnishing labor or materials — not from when payment was due. Public projects have bond claim deadlines instead, which are often shorter.

**Do lien rights apply to public projects?**
Mechanics liens cannot be filed against public property. Instead, subcontractors and suppliers on public projects have rights against the contractor's payment bond (required by the Miller Act on federal projects, and by state Little Miller Acts on state and local projects). Bond claim deadlines are often 90 days from last furnishing and must be strictly observed.

**What's the difference between conditional and unconditional lien waivers?**
A conditional waiver takes effect only when payment actually clears — if the check bounces, your lien rights are preserved. An unconditional waiver takes effect immediately upon signing, regardless of whether payment clears. Never sign an unconditional waiver until payment has fully cleared. Levelset and LienItNow both provide state-compliant waiver templates.

**Can a GC prevent subs from filing liens?**
A GC can require subs to sign no-lien agreements as a contract condition in some states, but many states prohibit these clauses as contrary to public policy. In California, Texas, and most other major construction states, no-lien clauses in private project subcontracts are unenforceable.

Para contratistas especializados y subcontratistas que trabajan en múltiples estados o en mercados de alto riesgo como California, Texas o Florida, Levelset ($699+/mes) es la mejor opción por su seguimiento automatizado de plazos y envío de avisos previos. Para contratistas que presentan gravámenes ocasionalmente, LienItNow ofrece preparación de documentos por presentación ($50-150) sin compromiso mensual.
