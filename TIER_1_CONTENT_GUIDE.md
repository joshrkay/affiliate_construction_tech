# Tier 1 Content Generation Guide

## Philosophy: Writing for Construction Workers

**Not hype. Not jargon. Not AI slop. Real advice that helps real contractors make decisions.**

This guide ensures every generated category page sounds like it was written by someone who understands construction, not by a marketing robot.

---

## Voice & Tone for Contractors

### Core Principles

**1. Be Practical, Not Theoretical**
- ❌ "Leverage cloud-based infrastructure to streamline workflows"
- ✅ "Get access to your job data from anywhere, on any device"

**2. Use Language Contractors Actually Use**
- ❌ "Facilitate real-time synchronization across stakeholders"
- ✅ "Your crew sees job changes instantly on their phones"

**3. Lead with the Outcome, Not the Feature**
- ❌ "Features include mobile time tracking with GPS"
- ✅ "Track exactly where your crew is and what they're working on"

**4. Be Specific About Money & Time**
- ❌ "Save significant time on administrative tasks"
- ✅ "Cut your paperwork from 6 hours a week to 30 minutes"

**5. Address Real Contractor Concerns**
- Cost (will it pay for itself?)
- Implementation (how long to set up?)
- Switching cost (is it worth leaving my current system?)
- Learning curve (do my guys need training?)

**6. No Exclamation Points**
- They feel fake and salesy
- Let the value speak for itself

**7. No Buzzwords Without Context**
Words to avoid or use carefully:
- "Streamline" (unless you explain HOW)
- "Innovative" (show what's innovative)
- "Cutting-edge" (irrelevant—will it help my business?)
- "Leverage," "facilitate," "optimize" (lazy synonyms)

## Content Structure for Category Pages

Every category page follows this structure, but writing must vary per category:

### 1. Introduction (150-200 words)

**Purpose:** Prove you understand their problem and explain why it matters.

**Format:**
- Open with the main pain point (in their words)
- Explain the business impact (time/money/stress)
- Briefly preview what the page covers
- No sales pitch yet

**Example (Estimating Software):**
```
Accurate estimates make or break construction jobs. Miss a cost, and your margin disappears. Underestimate labor, and your crew's working weekends for free. Yet contractors still spend hours manually counting items from blueprints, updating spreadsheets, and cross-checking numbers.

Estimating software automates this process. Instead of guessing, you get:
- Takeoff data extracted straight from plans
- Historical labor rates from actual jobs
- Automatic recalculation when specs change

The result? Estimates that are accurate, created 50% faster, and competitive enough to win jobs.
```

### 2. Why This Category Matters (100-150 words)

**Purpose:** Explain the business impact without sounding like marketing.

**Format:**
- Lead with a specific, measurable impact
- Explain the downstream effects
- Make it relatable to their business

**Example:**
```
The difference between an accurate estimate and a guess is your profit margin. Contractors who get estimating right typically improve job margins by 10-15%. That's $50,000-$150,000 per year for a mid-size contractor—money that comes straight to the bottom line.

Beyond profit, fast estimates mean faster quoting. If you can quote in a day instead of a week, you win more jobs. That's growth.
```

### 3. Key Features to Compare (200-250 words)

**Purpose:** Educate readers on what actually matters when choosing a tool.

**Format:**
- 4-5 features that matter for THIS category
- Explain why each matters (not just list features)
- Use examples that resonate

**Example:**
```
When evaluating estimating software, focus on:

**Digital Takeoff**: Instead of manually counting items from a PDF, the software extracts quantities automatically. This cuts estimate creation time in half and eliminates counting errors. Look for tools that let you import plans in multiple formats (PDF, images, CAD).

**Historical Labor Data**: The best estimates use actual data from your past jobs, not industry averages. The software should let you import or manually enter labor rates for your crew and trade. This makes estimates predictable and accurate.

**What-If Scenarios**: Projects change. You need to adjust material quantities or labor without re-creating the entire estimate. Good tools let you drag and drop items, change quantities, and see updated totals instantly.
```

### 4. Comparison Table (Varies)

**Purpose:** Let readers quickly see what each tool does.

**Format:**
- Show 3-4 top tools
- Compare 5-6 relevant features (not generic features)
- Use clear language (Yes/No or descriptions)
- Keep it honest (no exaggerating capabilities)

### 5. Tool Recommendations (150-200 words per tool, 3-4 tools)

**Purpose:** Recommend tools that fit different contractor types.

**Format:**
- Lead with who it's best for
- Explain why (specific reasons, not marketing)
- Mention pricing
- Be honest about limitations

**Example:**
```
**Procore for Large Commercial Projects**

Best for: General contractors managing $10M+ in annual projects

Why: Procore's digital takeoff is the strongest in the industry. It integrates with most CAD software and lets your estimators work in their native environment. For companies doing complex commercial projects with detailed specs, this is the gold standard.

Pricing: Custom pricing (typically $8,500+/year per user)

Limitation: Overkill for small residential contractors. The learning curve is steep if your team is used to spreadsheets.
```

### 6. FAQ Section (5-6 questions, 50-100 words each)

**Purpose:** Answer objections and uncertainties.

**Common FAQ patterns:**
- "Do I really need this software?"
- "How long does implementation take?"
- "Can I export my data if I switch?"
- "Do my crew need training?"
- "How much does it actually cost?"

**Format:** Direct answers, no fluff.

### 7. CTA (50 words)

**Purpose:** Give clear next step.

**Format:**
- Benefit-focused button text
- Brief supporting sentence
- No pressure

**Example:**
```
"Start your free trial" (get specific tool name)

See if this estimating approach works for your workflow before committing.
```

## How to Avoid "AI Slop"

### Red Flags to Remove

**Generic Openings:**
- ❌ "In today's fast-paced construction industry..."
- ❌ "The right software can transform your business..."
- ✅ "Accurate estimates are the difference between profit and loss."

**Lazy Comparisons:**
- ❌ "Each tool has its own strengths and weaknesses..."
- ✅ "Procore is best for large projects. Buildertrend is built for remodelers."

**No Numbers:**
- ❌ "Save significant time"
- ✅ "Save 6-8 hours per week"

**Meaningless Adjectives:**
- ❌ "innovative," "cutting-edge," "powerful," "world-class"
- ✅ Leave them out. Show, don't tell.

**Marketing Speak:**
- ❌ "Unlock new possibilities," "take your business to the next level"
- ✅ "Get accurate estimates," "move faster"

**Passive Voice:**
- ❌ "Estimates are generated in minutes"
- ✅ "You'll have an estimate in minutes"

### Quality Checklist Before Publishing

Every page should pass:

- [ ] Would a construction contractor think "yep, that's right" when they read this?
- [ ] Does it explain the "why" not just the "what"?
- [ ] Are there specific numbers (time saved, cost, margin improvement)?
- [ ] Did I use their language, not marketing language?
- [ ] Does the comparison table give honest trade-offs?
- [ ] Are CTAs clear and benefit-focused?
- [ ] Can I remove any paragraph without losing important info?

## Bilingual Content Strategy

### Content Structure for Spanish

**Language pairing:**
- Create English version first (main content)
- Spanish version follows (equal quality, not auto-translated)
- Both versions SEO-optimized for their language

**URL structure:**
```
/categories/project-estimating  (English)
/es/categories/project-estimating  (Spanish)
```

**Database schema:**
Each CategoryPage should have:
```typescript
{
  slug: string
  categoryId: string
  language: 'en' | 'es'
  
  // Content (varies by language)
  title: string
  metaDescription: string
  h1: string
  introduction: string
  sections: Section[]
  faqs: FAQ[]
  
  // Metadata
  published: boolean
  lastUpdated: string
}
```

### Translation Guidelines (Not Auto-Translation)

**Do NOT use Google Translate or auto-translation tools.** Spanish construction terminology is specific.

**Key construction terms in Spanish:**

| English | Spanish | Context |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Estimating software | Software de presupuestos | Not "estimación" alone |
| Crew scheduling | Programación de cuadrillas | "Cuadrilla" = field crew specifically |
| Field management | Gestión de obras | "Obra" = job site/project |
| Invoice | Factura | Not "invoicio" (not a word) |
| Job costing | Análisis de costos por obra | Specific to construction |
| Contractor | Contratista | Or "maestro" in some regions |
| ROI / Return | Retorno de inversión | Or just "ganancias" (profits) if clearer |

### Tone in Spanish

**Construction Spanish is direct and practical.** Don't make it flowery.

- ✅ "Ahorra 6 horas cada semana en presupuestos" (Save 6 hours each week on estimates)
- ❌ "Libérate del tedioso trabajo de presupuestos" (Overly poetic)

**Spanish pages should still avoid:**
- Buzzwords ("innovador," "revolucionario")
- Passive voice
- Generic openings

## Generation Workflow (Tier 1: 35 Categories)

### Step 1: Category Selection

**Priority order** (high search volume + high affiliate potential first):
1. `project-estimating`
2. `customer-invoicing`
3. `financial-management`
4. `field-crew-scheduling`
5. `project-scheduling`
... and so on (35 categories total)

### Step 2: Content Generation Per Category

**For each category, generate:**

1. **English version**
   - Run content generation prompts with contractor-focused guidelines
   - Review for AI slop
   - Manually enhance if needed

2. **Spanish version**
   - Translate key sections (don't auto-translate full page)
   - Adapt for Spanish construction terminology
   - Review for tone

3. **Database entries**
   - Create CategoryPage object for English
   - Create CategoryPage object for Spanish
   - Add both to `categoryContent.ts`

### Step 3: Quality Review Checklist

**Before publishing, verify:**
- [ ] No buzzwords or generic marketing language
- [ ] Specific numbers for time/cost/ROI
- [ ] Honest feature comparison (no exaggeration)
- [ ] CTA is clear and benefit-focused
- [ ] Spanish version uses construction terminology correctly
- [ ] Both versions are SEO-optimized for their language

### Step 4: Route & Build

- Add language routes to `app/routes.ts`
- Update `CategoryPage.tsx` to handle language parameter
- Run `npm run build` to pre-render all pages
- Verify bilingual pages in `dist/categories/` and `dist/es/categories/`
